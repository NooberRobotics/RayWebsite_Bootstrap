ENPH 253

This is an annual 5 credit course offered by UBC as part of the standard timetable of the Engineering Physics program. The students go together in groups of four and construct an autonomous robot to enter in a competition at the end of the term. The nature of the competition is different every year.

2016 competition

The competition of this year was set up as follows: traverse the roads marked with black tape (see map to the right), looking for passengers to pick up and transport to the drop-off area marked in gray. Potential passengers stand on the side of the curb on a base emitting IR light with a 1 kHz frequency. Two vehicles will be on the track at the same time. In the event of a collision, the robot must back away from the impact within 2 seconds. The robot that drops off the most dolls within two minutes wins the round.

Team 5, Noober Robotics

This website was made by the four students making up team 5 in the 2016 competition. The purpose of the website is to outline the scope of the project and some of the solutions we came up with in designing an autonomous system that was able to reliably meet the specified functionality. We are very proud of the result and hope visitors to the site appreciate the effort that has been required.

Design

The ENPH 253 course has, in addition to a heavy emphasis on hands-on lab-work, a technical communications component where conciseness, audience, and purpose is emphasized. These classes led us to identify three guiding principles of design that we have consciously strived to meet and to embody in the various stages of the project. They are as follows:

Robustness

Multiple systems co-existed in the robot, and having robust components enabled the system to come together and reliably navigate the track, handle collisions and pick up and drop off dolls.

Adaptability

Things change - rules, strategies and requirements set from other components have all required redesign of both mechanical systems and software. Thanks to an adaptable design we were able to handle these changes with a small turn-around time.

Simplicity

Consistently working out and implementing simple but effective solutions enabled us to finish the task on time. It kept us focused on the issue at hand.

CAD Design

Starting from scratch, SOLIDWORKS was an imporant tool in converting ideas to design. Detailed CAD-files of all parts ware made and assembled digitally to ensure smooth integration. That enabled us to have all components fit upon first assembly of the system, removing some overhead of mechanical redesign.

We went trough 2 major prototype-stages and various digital iterations. Upon completion, the digital library consisted of 46 distinct custom parts that we manufactured with digital precision and used in the final design.

Helpful SOLIDWORKS-features

Custom design library features were the foundation of all interlocking edges, as well motor attachments and other repeated features.

Design-tables were used for all dimensions to give mathematical, meaningful relations to all sides based on a few starting parameters. This also enabled us to make overarching dimensional changes in order to accommodate internal components in a matter of minutes rather than hours as it would have taken had the dimensions been hard-coded into every sketch.

Laser cutting

Deciding upon manufacturng-methods was an important step of the initial design-phase. It enabled us to do all modeling with manufacture in mind so that getting the models into real life would be possible. For the parts to be made on the laser cutter this entailed making each part be a flat sheet of thickness corresponding to the thickness of material available.

Material Choice

The chassis was made from either 3.175mm or 6.350mm thick hardboard. This choice was based on its favorouble price, ease of use and good strength to weight ratio.

The arm was made from acrylic, due to its favourable metrics of strength and durability in comparison to hardboard. Delrin was used for the main bracket that carries the gear as this proved to require even greater strength.

Waterjet cutting

The waterjet cutter was used to make the final bumper from thin sheet metal. The flexilbility of this material enabled the bumper to depress upon collision sufficiently to trip the collision-switches mounted behind.

3D Printing

Gears, fingers, wheels, spacer-brackets, slider-feet and battery-case were all manufactured using a ROBO 3D printer. This additive manufacturing technique enabled the combination of features into a single piece, minimizing the physical linkages required and allowing for a more compact design and increased reliability.

Sensor Systems

QRD, QSD and Touch Sensors

The QRD sensors operate by emitting IR light and measuring the amount reflected back. This allowed the robot to interpret the relative reflectance levels of the surface detected, thus allowing it to differentiate between black tape and the white streets of the course. These were connected directly into TINAH’s analog inputs. The Ray used four of these sensors, two for tape following and two for intersection detection.

The QSD sensors detected the infrared light emitted by the passengers. These were connected to specialized circuits which combined DC rectifying, filtering and amplification of the signal. The Ray has one of each of these sensors on each side to detect the prospective passengers on the course.

The Ray has four touch sensors on the front behind its metal bumper. This allows it to detect collision with the sidewalks and the opponent robot on the track.

IR Filtration

DC block, biquad Filtering and signal rectifying all in one

The IR circuits combined a DC block, a biquad filter, and a signal rectifier to produce an analog DC signal proportional to the strength of the signal detected.

The DC block removed the DC component of the signal. The biquad filter removes all signals that are not within a certain frequency range (tuned to detect 1 kHz). This circuit also amplifies the signal. The signal rectifier takes the output of the biquad filter to produce the final signal, it was also slightly modified to provide further amplification of the signal..

Borris

Connecting Everything Together

Borris was desinged as an alternative to wires. It allowed the Ray to have a clean internal electrical system. Furthermore, it minimized the possibilities for issues from loose connections.

Borris also allowed the IR circuits and H-bridges to be pluged in using a modular interface
